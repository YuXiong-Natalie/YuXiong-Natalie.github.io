Education Background

My research is broadly motivated by the question of how young children become a "Homo economicus" (the rational and consistent individual). To explore antecedents for children’s positive adjustment, I have been looking into parenting practice, children’s peer lives, and children’s time use. In my doctoral work, I study the role of early adolescents’ time use within and across social and academic domains in their psychosocial and academic adjustments.

Research Highlights

In my research, I mainly used a multi-method approach (i.e., survey, observation, experiment) and advanced longitudinal statistical methods (e.g., structural equation modeling, multi-level analysis) to examine the following topics: (1) parental-peer linkage in children’s psychosocial development during early adolescence (2) the role of parental goals and practices in shaping children’s emotional adjustment (3) influence of sociocultural contexts on these processes, and (4) coupling of the development of children’s motivational beliefs in math learning and math achievement. My first-author and second-author manuscripts addressing these topics have been published in top-tier journals (Developmental Psychology, Child Development, and Contemporary Educational Psychology).

Dissertaion Research

It is unclear whether children’s time use during early adolescence documented in the West extends beyond this region as the research to date has been limited to the West. Given that children’s family obligations and peer orientation have been argued to differ in the United States and China (Fuligni et al., 2002; Qu et al., 2016), examination of children’s time spent with family and friends is a key first step in understanding the universality of normative changes of children’s time use during early adolescence.

American and Chinese children’s time use (N = 934; mean age = 12.67 years) were measured three times in early adolescence (i.e., fall of 7th grade, spring of 7th grade, and fall of 8th grade). To obtain a broad understanding involving the effect of children’s time use in the United States and China, children’s reports of multiple dimensions of their adjustment, including emotional (αs >.92), social (αs >.74), and academic (αs > .73) adjustment, were examined.

Multiple indicator growth curve model that allows for partial scalar measurement invariance (Lai, 2021) will be used to examine how children’s time use changed during the early adolescent years in the United States and China. Two-group SEM analysis will be used to examine the role of time use in children’s adjustment over time. Children’s adjustment measured at T3 will be predicted from their time use at T1 and change in their time use over time, adjusting for their adjustment at T1.

The findings from this proposal will add to research on how culture shapes children’s time use during early adolescence and whether that plays a role in their optimal development. It will also set a foundation for future research seeking to understand children’s time use across social and academic domains.<\p>
